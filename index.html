Introduction

Wetlands are essential for maintaining ecological balance by supporting biodiversity, regulating water cycles, and improving water quality. In the U.S., many are managed by the Bureau of Land Management (BLM), which oversees over 245 million acres of public lands (Fig. 1). The BLM’s wetland and riparian monitoring programs, such as the National AIM Riparian and Wetland Indicators Hub, play a key role in sustaining ecosystem health. However, wetland condition and distribution vary greatly by geography, climate, and land use. Some states maintain diverse systems, while others face degradation from aridity or human pressures, posing challenges for equitable conservation planning.

This study analyzes BLM-managed wetlands across the western U.S. to identify areas needing greater conservation focus. Large regional disparities exist: Nevada holds the most wetlands, followed by California and Alaska, while Oregon has the fewest. These differences reflect climatic and geographic influences and highlight the need for region-specific conservation strategies to strengthen ecosystem resilience.

Methodology

Data from the BLM Natl AIM Riparian and Wetland Indicators Hub (Bureau of Land Management, 2025) was analyzed to assess the ecological diversity and health of wetlands across the contiguous U.S. All processing and visualization, including bar charts, heatmaps, and interactive mapping, were conducted in Python. Shannon entropy (H = −Σ pᵢ ln pᵢ) and its exponential, the Hill number (eᴴ), were used to measure true diversity among wetland types. The average C value served as a biological indicator of wetland integrity, where higher values represent less-disturbed ecosystems. By combining these metrics with visualization, this study highlights regional disparities and supports data-driven conservation planning.

Hydrogeomorphic (HGM) Characteristics

HGM Classes by Wetland Type

Figure 3 shows that Slope and Riverine wetlands dominate many western regions, indicating terrain-driven hydrology and strong surface flow influence. In contrast, Depression and Flat types are more common in lowland or coastal areas, reflecting greater water retention and limited drainage.

HGM Classes by State

Figure 4 highlights how HGM composition varies across states. Nevada, Utah, and Wyoming exhibit simpler, arid-adapted structures, while Oregon and Colorado show more balanced and diverse class distributions. These differences reflect how geomorphology and hydrology shape ecosystem function across landscapes.

Together, Figure 3 and 4 demonstrate that wetland geomorphology—and thus ecological function—differs significantly across regions. As a result, management and restoration strategies must be tailored to the hydrological regime and landscape characteristics of each state or wetland type.
